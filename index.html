Pontifex Maximus

The Pontifex Maximus was the title of the most powerful priest in ancient Rome. One of the important duties of the Pontifex Maximus was to regulate the calendar, both astronomically and in detailed application to the public life of the state. Since it was a elected political office this power was used to lengthen or shortening the year depending on if allies were in power.
One notable example of this was Julius Caesar1 who, elected Pontifex Maximus in 63 BCE, shifted the calendar to his own advantage to a degree that the crossing of Rubicon in January 49 BCE actually took place in mid-autumn. Julius Caesar was also the one that reformed the calendar creating the standard Julian calendar that was used until the Gregorian reform in the 16th century.
